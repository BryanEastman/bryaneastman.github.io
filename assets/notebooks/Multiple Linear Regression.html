Linear Regression Walkthrough¶

The purpose of this project is to provide a walkthrough of a simple linear regression, including the initial exploration of data through interpretation of diagnostics. The dataset chosen is simple, clean, and easily interpreted for the sake of demonstrating the techniques used in LR as opposed to using a lot of energy to clean messy data or model complex interactions.

Diagnostics¶

Residuals vs Fitted¶

The first diagnostic is the Residuals vs. Fitted graph, which visualizes how residuals (difference between predicted and actual values) vary across the fitted values (predicted values). This helps us to verify the assumptions of homoscedasticity (variance is the same for all variables), linearity, and normality.

With this graph we are looking for a random distribution of points in the scatterplot with no apparent funelling or clear patterns.

In [22]:
%matplotlib inline
fitted = mod_fit.fittedvalues

sns.residplot(
    y=y,
    x=fitted,
    lowess=True,
    line_kws={'color':'red'}
)

plt.show()

It looks like there is a small funneling pattern but this is not large or clear enough to be of concern, mostly drawn up by the values at the far ends. We can further test this using a Breusch-Pagan test.

The null hypothesis for the Breusch-Pagan test is that data is homoscedastic, a significant result would indicate inequal variance for predicted values and residuals.

Normal Q-Q¶

This plot compares the distribution of residuals against a theoretical distribution (in this case, the normal distribution, as we are interested in seeing whether residuals are normally distributed). Visually, this would mean the plot of normalized residuals follows a 45 degree line, or there are no apparent deviations from a straight line.

In [24]:

The Q-Q plot above shows good evidence for normal distribution of residuals.

Residuals vs. Leverage¶

This plot shows changes in all estimates when high-leverage values are removed, utilizing Cook's distance (represented by the dashed lines). Any points that fall outside of Cook's distance are considered "influential", meaning their removal would significantly impact the results of the model. Points further along the x-axis have greater leverage, or model influence. Points with greater absolute standardized residuals tend to indicate poor fits.

In our plot, we notice a number of high leverage points falling beyond Cook's distance, indicating problematic levels of influence. Furthermore, many of the points are poorly fit by the existing model, as evidenced by the high absolute-value normalized residuals. We are already aware that the model is a pretty poor fit given the low $R^2$ value, this provides further evidence for the point.

VIF¶

Lastly, we can check for multicollinearity using the Variance Inflation Factor which examines correlation among 3 or more factors. General rules when interpreting are:

  • VIF > 5: High Collinearity
  • VIF < 1: Little to No Collinearity

Final Model¶

To recap what we have seen so far, our data does not violate assumptions of normality or homogeneity of variance. Though the predictors have low correlation values among each other, there is strong evidence for multicollinearity using the VIF score. Lastly, the Year 1 score is likely removable from the the model because it is a poor predictor.

Using the above information, a new model can be constructed and tested for accuracy. Our new model will:

  • Drop Yr1 for low correlation to the outcome
  • Model the interaction between the remaining predictors (Yr2 moderating Yr3)

X2 = X.drop(columns='Yr1')
X2y = X2.join(y)

mod2 = smf.ols(formula='Final ~ Yr2 + Yr3 + Yr2 * Yr3', data=X2y)

res2 = mod2.fit()
