Notes on limitations of the dataset¶

Inspecting some of the other values, we see some more nonsense data. For example, the EmployeeStatus field has a value of Future Start for employees with both a valid start and end date. Furthermore, Active candidates have a Termination value. This field is not worth cleaning, instead we should verify that the ExitDate and TerminationType are sensible, because the same information is inferred from these values as we would get from EmployeeStatus. TerminationDescription seem to just be filler text and is unusable as well. Lastly, EmployeeType and EmployeeClassificationType are seemingly contradictory in determinng Full and Part-Time employees. We'll just pick EmployeeType for the sake of simplicity. Though we might generally be interested in state-by-state differences, because it will add a large number of classes and is tedious for the sake of example, we'll drop State as well and just treat PayZone to mean the region. JobFunctionDescription contradicts the Title, and BusinessUnit Does not appear useable.

Engineering Features¶

Now that we have cleaned out some of the value-less features, we can derive additional information. For example, we might be interested in the age and tenure of employees leaving the org. In some scenarios, it might inform us that an aging workforce puts the org at risk of high attrition due to retirement, for example (though based on what we've seen from the earlier data, it's likely this was randomly generated and I'm anticipating seeing retirees in young groups, maybe our org has a lot of lottery winners). For the sake of this analysis, we will take the last date in the dataset and set the "current date" for our calculations.

Correlations¶

We next will look at correlations between the features and turnover. We have a combination of nominal, ordinal, and continuous IV, and a binary DV (whether or not the employee left).

We will use the appropriate tests for these combinatons:

continuous:

  • Age
  • Tenure

ordinal:

  • Performance Score
  • Current Employee Rating

All the rest are nominal, which will require further transformation if the class sizes are too large to meaningfully interpret.

For the continuous IV, dichotomous DV, we will use Point-Biserial correlation. For ordinal IV, dichotomous DV, we use Rank-Biserial.

Discussion¶

While we see a significant result, further digging would be needed to determine if there is an action to take. Though higher tenure is predictive of attrition, it is clear from the quartile distribution of tenure to attrition that it would not be enough to make any informed business decision. Unfortunately, this dataset was very low quality and had poor design of variables which makes it difficult for any sort of predictive analysis. This notebook should just serve as an example of a walkthrough EDA on HR data and the types of techniques used to understand data. Moving forward, I'll be looking for non-synthetic data for deeper dives, or at least higher quality datasets from more trusted sources.
